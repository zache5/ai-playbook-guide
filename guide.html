    Part 1: Understanding AI and Claude

    Everything in this section uses claude.ai in your web browser. No downloads, no terminal, no code. If you never go beyond these chapters, you can still save significant time every week.

    Chapter 1: How AI Got Here

    You do not need a computer science degree to understand where AI came from. But knowing the basics gives you a massive advantage: you will understand why these tools work the way they do, why they fail the way they do, and why right now is the single best time in history to learn this.

    If you have never used AI before, welcome. This chapter is your starting point. If you have been using ChatGPT or Claude for a while, you will still find useful context here about what is happening across the industry and where things are heading. Either way, this chapter gives you the foundation that makes everything else in the guide click.

    The five eras in 60 seconds

    Era 1: Rule-Based Systems (1950s-1980s). Early AI was just if/then rules written by humans. "If the customer says X, respond with Y." These systems were brittle. They could only handle situations someone had explicitly programmed. Think of early phone tree systems or chess programs that followed scripted moves.

    Era 2: Machine Learning (1990s-2000s). Instead of writing rules, engineers started feeding data to algorithms and letting them find patterns. Spam filters are a classic example: the system learned what spam looks like by seeing millions of emails. Better than rules, but still limited to narrow tasks.

    Era 3: Deep Learning (2010s). Neural networks with many layers ("deep" networks) started beating humans at image recognition, language translation, and game playing. Google Translate got noticeably better. Siri and Alexa became possible. But each system still did one thing.

    Era 4: Transformers (2017-2022). In 2017, Google researchers published a paper called "Attention Is All You Need." It introduced the transformer architecture, which is the foundation of every major AI tool you use today. Transformers can process entire sequences of text at once instead of word by word, which made them dramatically faster and better at understanding context.

    Era 5: Large Language Models (2022-present). Transformers scaled up to billions of parameters, trained on enormous amounts of text. The result: AI that can write, reason, code, analyze, and converse. This is where Claude, ChatGPT, and Gemini live.

    The milestones that got us here

    • 2018: GPT-1 by OpenAI. A proof of concept. 117 million parameters. Could generate semi-coherent text but nothing useful for real work.
    • 2020: GPT-3. 175 billion parameters. The first model that could write essays, code, and have conversations that felt almost human. The tech world took notice.
    • 2021: DALL-E. OpenAI showed that AI could generate images from text descriptions. "An astronaut riding a horse in the style of Andy Warhol" actually worked.
    • November 2022: ChatGPT. OpenAI wrapped GPT-3.5 in a free chat interface. It reached 100 million users in two months, the fastest-growing consumer app in history. This is the moment AI went mainstream.
    • March 2023: GPT-4. A major leap in reasoning, accuracy, and capability. It could pass the bar exam, write complex code, and handle nuanced instructions.
    • March 2024: Claude 3. Anthropic released Claude 3 Opus, Sonnet, and Haiku. Claude 3 Opus matched or exceeded GPT-4 on many benchmarks, with a focus on safety and helpfulness. The 200K context window was a standout feature.
    • 2025: Claude 3.5 and Claude 4. Claude 3.5 Sonnet became the go-to model for coding and analysis. Claude 4 Opus and Sonnet introduced extended thinking and better reasoning. Claude Code launched as a full agentic coding tool.
    • 2026: Claude 4.5 and 4.6. Opus 4.6 and Sonnet 4.6 arrived with 1M token context windows, Claude Code Routines for cloud automation, Computer Use, Managed Agents, and MCP crossing 97 million installs. The gap between "AI assistant" and "AI employee" got very small.
    • 2025-2026: The agent era. AI moved from answering questions to doing work. Claude Code, GitHub Copilot agent mode, and similar tools can now plan, execute, and iterate on complex tasks across your entire computer.

    The speed of change, in perspective

    The internet took about 7 years to reach 100 million users. The smartphone took about 5 years. ChatGPT did it in 2 months. Claude reached millions of users within its first year.

    In 2020, AI could write a passable paragraph. By 2024, it could build a working web application, pass professional exams, and analyze a 200-page document in seconds. By 2026, it can run 24/7 on your computer, connect to your email, calendar, and files, and execute multi-step tasks while you sleep.

    That is six years. The pace is not slowing down.

    Where we are now: what AI can actually do in 2026

    Let us be specific about what AI can and cannot do today. Not the marketing pitch. Not the doomsday prediction. The actual reality.

    Text and writing

    AI can write emails, essays, reports, marketing copy, creative fiction, code documentation, social media posts, business plans, legal summaries, and virtually any form of written content. The quality ranges from decent to excellent depending on how well you prompt it. It can match your voice and style if you give it examples. It can translate between dozens of languages. It can summarize a 200-page document in seconds.

    Code and software

    AI can write code in nearly every programming language. It can build websites, create applications, write scripts, debug errors, and explain what code does in plain English. Claude Code takes this further by actually running code on your computer and building complete projects. You do not need to know how to code. You describe what you want, and it builds it.

    Images and visual content

    Tools like DALL-E (OpenAI), Midjourney, and Stable Diffusion can generate images from text descriptions. Want a logo, a product mockup, an illustration, or a social media graphic? Describe it and it appears. Quality has gone from "interesting experiment" to "professional grade" in about two years. AI can also analyze and describe images you upload, read text in photos, and extract data from charts.

    Video and audio

    AI video generation is advancing rapidly. Tools like Sora (OpenAI), Runway, and Kling can generate short video clips from text prompts. AI voice tools like ElevenLabs can clone voices, generate realistic speech, and create voiceovers. These are not perfect yet, but they are improving fast and already useful for certain applications.

    Reasoning and analysis

    Modern AI models can solve complex math problems, analyze data sets, evaluate arguments, plan multi-step projects, and reason through difficult questions. Claude and GPT-4o class models can pass professional exams including the bar exam, medical licensing exams, and graduate-level tests. They can review contracts, identify patterns in data, and provide strategic analysis.

    What AI cannot do (honest version)

    • It cannot know what happened today. AI models are trained on data up to a certain date. Unless connected to the internet (which requires setup), they do not know about current events, recent prices, or breaking news.
    • It cannot guarantee accuracy. AI can and will confidently state things that are wrong. This is called hallucination. It is not malicious, it is a fundamental limitation of how these systems work. Always verify important claims.
    • It cannot replace human judgment. AI gives you information and options. The decision is still yours. It does not know your full context, your relationships, or the nuances of your situation the way you do.
    • It cannot access your accounts. Unless you specifically connect it (through tools like MCP servers, covered later in this guide), AI cannot see your email, your bank account, your files, or anything else on your computer.
    • It cannot feel or understand. Despite how natural the conversations feel, AI does not have emotions, consciousness, or understanding in the way humans do. It processes patterns in text. Very sophisticated patterns, but patterns nonetheless.
    • It cannot do physical tasks. AI cannot make a phone call for you, drive your car, or cook your dinner. It operates in the digital world only.

    Chapter 2: What Claude Actually Is (And Is Not)

    Before you personalize Claude or start using power tricks, you need to understand what this tool actually is. Not the hype. Not the fear. Just the reality.

    Claude is not a search engine

    When you type something into Google, it matches keywords and shows you links to web pages. Google does not understand your question. It matches words.

    Claude is different. When you type something, it reads your entire message, understands the intent behind it, and writes a response from scratch. It does not look up web pages. It generates an answer based on patterns it learned during training on a massive amount of text.

    Claude is not magic

    Claude is very good at:

    • Writing and editing text in any style or format
    • Analyzing documents, data, and complex information
    • Explaining hard topics in simple terms
    • Brainstorming ideas and strategies
    • Writing code (even though you do not need to know code to benefit)
    • Following detailed, specific instructions

    And it has real limitations:

    • It does not access the internet in real-time (unless you set that up later)
    • It sometimes states things confidently that are wrong (hallucination)
    • It does not remember previous conversations unless you set up a system for that
    • It has a knowledge cutoff date, so it may not know about very recent events

    The three ways to use Claude

    1. Claude.ai (the browser chat) is where you start. Go to claude.ai, create an account, and start typing. This is what most people think of as "Claude."

    2. Claude Code (the agent on your computer) is where things get powerful. It runs in your terminal and can actually do things: create files, build websites, organize folders, connect to email, and much more.

    • Claude.ai is like texting a brilliant friend for advice.
    • Claude Code is like hiring that friend to come to your house and do the work.

    3. Claude API is for software developers building Claude into their own apps. If you are not a developer, skip this entirely.

    Chapter 3: Make Claude Yours

    Most people open Claude, type a question, get a generic answer, and think that is the whole experience. They are wrong. The single most important thing you can do with Claude is not asking your first question. It is telling Claude who you are.

    Now that you understand where AI came from (Chapter 1) and what Claude actually is (Chapter 2), this chapter makes everything that follows better. A realtor asking Claude for help with a listing gets a different answer than a college student asking for writing help. But only if Claude knows the difference.

    Right now, Claude knows nothing about you. It does not know your job. It does not know your family situation. It does not know whether you are a plumber in Houston or a retired teacher in Maine. Every response it gives you is based on guessing who you are. That guessing ends here.

    The personalization prompt

    At the start of a new conversation, before you ask Claude to do anything, paste in a message like this:

    Your personalization prompt
    Before we start, here is some context about me so you can tailor your responses: Who I am: [Your name, what you do, where you are] What I care about: [Your priorities, goals, what you are working on] How I communicate: [Your preferred tone, formality level, pet peeves] My experience level: [What you know well, what you are new to] What I use Claude for: [Your main use cases] Keep this in mind for the rest of our conversation.

    That template is a starting point. The real magic is in making it specific to your life. Here is what that looks like for different people.

    For Business Owners
    As a business owner, this personalization step is where you will see the biggest ROI from Claude. Include your business name, industry, pricing, team size, and the specific tasks that eat your time. The more Claude knows about your operation, the faster it works.
    For Students
    For school, include your major, courses, writing style preferences, and what format your professors expect (APA, MLA, Chicago). This one step means every paper, study guide, and project gets the right context automatically.
    For Personal Use
    At home, this works great for setting up Claude as your household assistant. Include family details, dietary restrictions, budgets, and the kinds of tasks you need help with: meal planning, budgeting, scheduling, or family coordination.
    For Healthcare Workers
    In healthcare settings, include your role, specialty, shift schedule, and what you need help with most: documentation, research summaries, continuing education, or communication with colleagues and patients.
    For Creatives
    As a creative professional, include your medium, style, target audience, and brand voice. Claude adapts to your aesthetic sensibility when it knows whether you are a minimalist designer or a bold content creator.

    Chapter 4: When to Implement AI

    Before we go any further, we need to talk about something most guides ignore. If you have ever felt a twinge of guilt about using AI, this chapter is for you. You are not alone. Research shows 36% of Gen Z workers feel guilty about using AI, concerned about authenticity and effort.

    Let us deal with that right now.

    AI is a tool.

    When calculators first appeared in classrooms, people panicked. "Students will never learn math." Calculators did not make accountants lazy. They let accountants do more complex, more valuable work.

    When spell-check arrived, nobody called writers cheaters. When Google replaced encyclopedias, nobody said researchers were cutting corners. When Excel replaced hand-drawn ledgers, nobody told finance teams they were being dishonest.

    AI is the next step in that same progression. It is a tool that handles the mechanical parts of thinking so you can focus on the parts that actually require you: judgment, creativity, context, and decisions.

    When AI use IS appropriate

    Chapter 5: AI Literacy Fundamentals

    You do not need to understand how a car engine works to drive. But knowing what the check engine light means, what happens when you run low on oil, and why you should not drive on an empty tank will save you a lot of trouble. This chapter is your AI "driver's ed."

    What is a model?

    Think of an AI model as the brain. It is not a program in the traditional sense. It is a massive mathematical pattern that was trained by reading enormous amounts of text. Nobody programmed specific answers into it. It learned patterns from data, the way you learned language by hearing millions of sentences as a kid.

    Different models have different abilities, like different brains. Claude's brain (made by Anthropic) is different from ChatGPT's brain (made by OpenAI) or Gemini's brain (made by Google). They were trained differently, on different data, with different priorities.

    What are tokens and why you should care

    When you send a message to Claude, it does not read words. It reads "tokens," which are roughly 3/4 of a word each. "I want to learn about artificial intelligence" is about 7 tokens.

    Why does this matter? Because tokens cost money. Every plan has a token limit, which controls how many messages you can send. The more tokens in your message (and Claude's response), the more of your allowance you use. This is why:

    • Clear, concise prompts are better than rambling ones
    • Starting a new conversation when the old one gets long saves you tokens
    • Pasting an entire 50-page document uses more of your allowance than summarizing what you need

    What is a context window (the whiteboard analogy)

    Imagine Claude has a whiteboard. Everything you say and everything Claude says gets written on this whiteboard. The whiteboard is massive (up to 1 million tokens, roughly 700,000 words for Claude), but not infinite.

    When the whiteboard fills up, the oldest content starts getting erased. This is why long conversations eventually degrade: Claude literally cannot see your earlier instructions anymore. Signs this is happening:

    • Claude forgets what you told it earlier
    • Responses become generic or repetitive
    • Claude contradicts itself

    The fix is simple: start a new conversation. This is not broken. It is how context windows work.

    What is hallucination and how to catch it

    AI "hallucination" is when Claude (or any AI) confidently states something that is wrong. It is not lying. It does not know the difference between true and false. It is generating text that sounds right based on patterns, and sometimes those patterns produce fiction.

    Hallucinations are most common when:

    • You ask about very recent events (after the training data cutoff)
    • You ask about specific numbers, dates, or statistics
    • You ask about niche topics with limited training data
    • You ask for citations or sources

    We cover how to verify AI output in detail in Chapter 8.

    Fine-tuning, RAG, and agents (30 seconds each)

    Fine-tuning: Taking a general AI model and training it further on specific data so it gets better at one thing. Like hiring a general assistant and then training them specifically on your business. You will never need to do this yourself.

    RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation): Instead of relying only on what the AI learned during training, RAG lets it look things up in a database first, then answer. It is like giving Claude access to a filing cabinet before it responds. This is what MCP servers do (Chapter 18).

    Agents: AI that does not just answer questions but takes actions. Claude Code is an agent. It reads files, creates documents, runs commands, and builds things. Most chatbots are not agents. Claude Code is.

    Chapter 8: How to Verify AI Output

    AI will be wrong sometimes. The skill is not avoiding mistakes. It is catching them before they matter.

    The 3-check method

    Every time Claude gives you information that matters, run it through three quick checks:

    1. Does it sound right? Trust your expertise. If you are a nurse and Claude says something about a medication that does not match your training, flag it. Your domain knowledge is your first filter.
    2. Can I verify the key claim? Pick the most important fact in the response and check it independently. Google the statistic. Look up the law. Confirm the date. You do not need to verify everything. Just the load-bearing claims.
    3. Would I stake my reputation on this? If you are sending it to a client, your boss, or putting it in a presentation, it needs to be right. If the answer to this question is "I would not bet on it," verify before using it.

    When Claude is most likely to be wrong

    • Specific numbers and statistics. Claude sometimes invents plausible-sounding stats. Always verify exact numbers.
    • Recent events. Anything after Claude's training cutoff may be wrong or missing entirely.
    • Citations and sources. Claude can fabricate realistic-looking citations. Always check that a cited source actually exists.
    • Legal and medical specifics. General principles are usually solid. Specific laws, codes, drug interactions, and dosages must be verified by a professional.
    • Niche or local information. The more specialized and less commonly written about, the higher the hallucination risk.

    How to ask Claude to cite sources

    Requesting citations
    For every claim you make, note whether you are confident in the accuracy. If I should verify something independently, tell me. Do not make up citations. If you are not sure of a source, say so.

    Claude is more honest about uncertainty when you explicitly give it permission to say "I am not sure."

    Red flags in AI output

    • Suspiciously perfect statistics (e.g., "studies show a 73.2% improvement")
    • Specific citations you have never heard of
    • Confident claims about very recent events
    • Information that contradicts what it said earlier in the same conversation
    • Overly precise numbers where approximations would be more realistic
    60-second verification checklist
    Before using any AI output for anything that matters: (1) Read it completely, not just skim. (2) Identify the single most important claim. (3) Spend 30 seconds verifying that claim independently. (4) If it checks out, you are probably fine. If it does not, re-examine everything.

    Chapter 9: Claude's Limitations (And When to Use Other Tools)

    Every guide you have read about AI tells you what it can do. This chapter tells you what it cannot do, where it struggles, and when you should use something else entirely. This will save you hours of frustration and make you a smarter user.

    What Claude cannot do

    • Browse the internet in chat. When you talk to Claude on claude.ai, it cannot look things up online. It works from its training data only. If you need real-time information (stock prices, today's news, current weather), Claude does not have it unless you provide it or set up web access through MCP (Chapter 18).

    • Access real-time data. Claude's training data has a cutoff date. It does not know about events that happened after that date unless you tell it. Always verify time-sensitive information independently.
    • Remember across sessions (without setup). Each new conversation starts fresh. Claude does not remember what you talked about yesterday unless you use Projects on claude.ai or CLAUDE.md files with Claude Code (Chapters 2 and 17).
    • Run software on its own. Regular Claude (the chat) cannot execute code, access files, or interact with your computer. Claude Code can, but the chat interface cannot.

    Where Claude struggles

    • Precise math. Claude can reason about math concepts, but it sometimes makes arithmetic errors on complex calculations. For anything financial or mission-critical, verify the numbers with a calculator or spreadsheet.
    • Very recent events. Anything that happened after Claude's training cutoff is unknown territory. Claude will sometimes try to answer anyway, which leads to confident-sounding wrong answers.
    • Highly specialized niche domains. Claude knows a lot about a lot, but it is not an expert in extremely specialized fields like rare medical conditions, obscure legal jurisdictions, or very recent research published last month.
    • Very long documents. Claude now supports up to 1 million tokens of context (roughly 700,000 words), which is enormous. But even with this massive window, performance can degrade toward the edges, and very large inputs still cost more tokens from your plan allowance.
    • Counting and spatial reasoning. Ask Claude how many times the letter "r" appears in "strawberry" and it might get it wrong. These seem like easy tasks, but they are actually hard for language models.

    Context window limits explained practically

    Think of Claude's context window as a massive whiteboard that holds up to 1 million tokens (roughly 700,000 words). Everything you say, everything Claude says, and any documents you upload all go on this whiteboard. When it fills up, the oldest content falls off.

    In practice, this means:

    • A typical conversation can go for hours before hitting limits
    • Uploading a 50-page document uses roughly 15,000-20,000 words of your window
    • Long coding sessions in Claude Code can approach limits after extended back-and-forth
    • When you notice Claude "forgetting" things you said earlier, you may have hit the edge

    Compaction (2026): Claude now handles this automatically. When a conversation approaches the context limit, it compresses older messages into a summary so the conversation can keep going. You do not need to do anything to enable this. In practice, this means conversations can continue far longer than the raw token limit suggests. If you still notice quality dropping in a very long session, starting fresh is the backup plan.

    • Free plan: The most restrictive. You may hit limits after 10-20 messages depending on length.
    • Pro plan ($20/month): Significantly more usage, but you can still hit limits during heavy sessions.
    • Max plan ($100-200/month): Much higher limits. Designed for heavy daily use.
    • Claude Code: Included with Pro and Max plans. Usage counts against your plan's message allowance, with optional extra usage available at API rates.

    Chapter 25: Security and Privacy

    Important
    This chapter is educational information, not legal advice. If you have specific compliance questions for your organization, consult a qualified professional.

    AI tools are powerful, but they come with real privacy and security considerations. This chapter covers what you need to know to use Claude (and any AI) responsibly.

    What data does Claude see and store?

    When you send a message to Claude, Anthropic's servers process your text to generate a response. What happens after that depends on your plan:

    • Free and Pro plans: Your conversations may be used to improve the model unless you opt out in Settings > Privacy.
    • Max, Team, and Enterprise plans: Your data is excluded from training by default.
    • Claude Code: Your files and terminal context are sent to Anthropic's servers for processing. On Pro and above, you can manage data retention settings.
    • All plans: Anthropic retains conversation data for up to 30 days for safety review, then deletes it (per their current policy). Verify current policies at anthropic.com.

    Should you connect personal apps (Gmail, Calendar)?

    Connecting apps like Gmail and Google Calendar to Claude gives you powerful functionality: reading emails, drafting responses, checking your schedule, and more. But consider:

    Benefits:

    • Dramatically faster email management and scheduling
    • Automated morning briefings and task summaries
    • Hands-free calendar management

    Risks to consider:

    • Claude can see the content of your emails and calendar events
    • If your Claude account is compromised, an attacker could potentially access connected services
    • Sensitive information in emails (financial details, health information, legal matters) would be processed by Claude's servers

    Recommendation: Connect personal accounts only if you are on a Pro plan or higher and have reviewed your privacy settings. Consider creating a separate email alias for AI-managed communications if privacy is a high priority for you.

    What if your account gets hacked?

    If someone gains access to your Claude account:

    • They could see your conversation history
    • If you have connected apps, they could potentially access those services
    • Any API keys or credentials shared in conversations could be exposed

    Protect yourself:

    • Use a strong, unique password for your Anthropic account
    • Enable two-factor authentication if available
    • Never paste passwords, API keys, or credentials into conversations
    • Regularly review your connected apps and remove ones you no longer use
    • If you suspect compromise, change your password immediately and disconnect all apps

    How to use Claude safely with sensitive information

    Some information is fine to share with Claude. Some is not. Here is a practical breakdown:

    Generally safe to share:

    • General business questions and strategy
    • Public information you are asking Claude to analyze
    • Your own writing for editing
    • General descriptions of your work and projects

    Share with caution (anonymize when possible):

    • Client details (use "Client A" instead of real names)
    • Internal business data (consider whether it is truly necessary)
    • Employee information
    • Financial figures that are not public

    What NOT to share with any AI

    Never paste any of the following into Claude or any other AI tool:

    • Passwords for any account
    • Social Security numbers (yours or anyone else's)
    • Credit card numbers or bank account numbers
    • Financial account credentials (brokerage logins, crypto keys)
    • API keys or secret tokens (use environment variables instead)
    • Medical record numbers or protected health information
    • Legal case files with identifying client information
    • Student records with personally identifiable information

    Business data considerations

    If you use Claude for work, ask yourself: "Would my company be comfortable knowing this data was processed by a third-party AI?" If the answer is no, do not paste it.

    • Check your company's AI usage policy before using Claude with work data
    • Use Team or Enterprise plans for business use (your data is not used for training)
    • Anonymize client data before sharing it with Claude
    • Never share proprietary algorithms, trade secrets, or unreleased product details

    Compliance considerations (HIPAA, FERPA, and others)

    If you work in a regulated industry, AI use has additional considerations:

    • HIPAA (Healthcare): Protected Health Information (PHI) should not be entered into consumer AI tools. If you need AI for healthcare workflows, use enterprise-grade solutions with a Business Associate Agreement (BAA).
    • FERPA (Education): Student education records and personally identifiable information from those records should not be shared with consumer AI tools. Use anonymized data or institutional-approved AI tools.
    • Financial regulations: Client financial data, trading information, and account details are subject to various regulations. Check with your compliance team before using AI with any client data.
    • Legal privilege: Attorney-client privileged information shared with a third-party AI tool could potentially waive that privilege. Consult your bar association's guidance on AI use.

    Best practices for data hygiene

    1. Review your privacy settings on every AI tool you use, today.
    2. Start new conversations for unrelated topics (do not mix sensitive and non-sensitive topics).
    3. Anonymize before pasting. Replace real names, account numbers, and identifying details with placeholders.
    4. Delete conversations that contain sensitive information after you are done with them.
    5. Audit your connected apps quarterly. Remove anything you are not actively using.
    6. Use the right plan level. If you handle sensitive data professionally, a Max or Team plan with training-data exclusion is worth the cost.
    7. Keep AI tools updated. Security patches matter.

    Fix: Wait a few minutes. Combine questions into fewer messages.

    Fix: Start a new conversation. Use /compact to summarize and free space.

    Fix: Rephrase with more context about the legitimate purpose. Be direct about what you need and why.

    Transport error

    Fix: Remove and re-add the server. Restart Claude Code.

    Fix: Regenerate key. Check for extra spaces. Verify permissions.

    Glossary: AI Terms in Plain English

    Every term you need, explained without jargon. Use the search box to find specific terms.

    Agent
    An AI that does not just answer questions but takes actions. Claude Code is an agent because it can create files, run commands, and interact with services on your behalf.
    API (Application Programming Interface)
    A way for one piece of software to talk to another. When Claude connects to Gmail, it uses Gmail's API. Think of it as a phone line between two services.
    API Key
    A password that lets one service access another. Treat it like a password: do not share it or post it publicly.
    Anthropic
    The company that makes Claude. Founded by former OpenAI researchers. Based in San Francisco.
    CLAUDE.md
    A text file that Claude Code reads at the start of every session. It contains your preferences, instructions, and context so Claude remembers who you are.
    Claude Code
    An AI agent that runs in your terminal and can create files, build projects, connect to services, and automate tasks on your computer.
    Context Window
    The amount of text an AI can "see" at once. Like a whiteboard that fills up. When full, the oldest content gets erased. Claude's is up to 1 million tokens (roughly 700,000 words).
    Cron
    A scheduling system used to run tasks at specific times. "0 8 * * 1-5" means "every weekday at 8am." Claude's /schedule command handles this for you in plain English.
    Fine-tuning
    Training a general AI model on specific data to make it better at one thing. Like hiring a generalist and then training them on your industry. You will never need to do this yourself.
    GPT (Generative Pre-trained Transformer)
    The type of AI architecture ChatGPT uses. A technical term for a specific approach to building language models. Claude uses a similar but distinct approach.
    Hallucination
    When AI confidently states something that is wrong. It is not lying. It is generating text that sounds right based on patterns, but the patterns produced fiction. Always verify important claims.
    LLM (Large Language Model)
    The general term for AI systems like Claude, ChatGPT, and Gemini. "Large" refers to the number of parameters (billions). "Language" because they process text. "Model" because they are statistical models of language.
    MCP (Model Context Protocol)
    A system that lets Claude connect to external services like Gmail, Calendar, and websites. Think of MCP servers as plugins.
    Model
    The AI "brain." A massive mathematical pattern trained on text data. Different models (Claude Opus, Claude Sonnet, GPT-4o) have different strengths.
    Node.js
    A program that lets JavaScript code run on your computer (instead of just in a web browser). Claude Code needs it the way a car needs an engine.
    npm
    Node Package Manager. A tool for installing software packages (like Claude Code). Comes bundled with Node.js.
    Opus
    Claude's most powerful model. Best for complex reasoning, nuanced writing, and difficult problems. Uses more of your message allowance than Sonnet.
    Parameter
    A single tunable value in an AI model. Modern models have billions of parameters. More parameters generally means more capability, but also more cost to run.
    Project (Claude)
    A feature in claude.ai that stores instructions and context. Every conversation inside a project automatically includes those instructions.
    Prompt
    The text you send to an AI. Everything you type in the message box is your prompt. Better prompts produce better results.
    Prompt Engineering
    The skill of writing effective prompts to get useful AI output. What most of this guide teaches you.
    RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation)
    A technique where AI looks up information in a database before answering, instead of relying only on training data. Like giving Claude a filing cabinet to reference.
    Routines
    Claude Code automations that run on Anthropic's cloud without your computer. Triggered on a schedule, via API, or by GitHub events. Available on paid plans as a research preview (April 2026).
    Rate Limit
    The maximum number of messages or tokens you can use in a given time period. Hit it, and you wait. Higher plans have higher limits.
    Sonnet
    Claude's everyday model. Fast, capable, and efficient. Good for most tasks. The default when you open claude.ai.
    SSH (Secure Shell)
    A way to connect to another computer remotely through the terminal. Used to access a Mac Mini server from your laptop or phone.
    Terminal
    The text-based interface to your computer. Instead of clicking icons, you type commands. Claude Code runs in the terminal.
    tmux
    A tool that keeps terminal sessions running even after you disconnect. Essential for running Claude Code 24/7 on a server.
    Token
    The basic unit AI processes. Roughly 3/4 of a word. "Hello world" is 2 tokens. Tokens determine cost and limits.
    Training Data
    The text an AI model learned from. Claude was trained on books, articles, websites, code, and conversations. It does not memorize this data; it learns patterns from it.
    Training Cutoff
    The date after which the AI has no knowledge. If something happened after the cutoff, Claude may not know about it or may have incorrect information.
